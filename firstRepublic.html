Dolce Vita (1960)


Rock-and-Roll

During the 50s, the entire world became enchanted by Rock and Roll. Often this style of dance mostly had to do with the music but often included the dance style as well (like the Jitterbug) (Watson). Italy was no exception to this mentality. Though these dances were often deemed controversial because of the swinging hips, youth loved it and could not get enough. Elvis seemed to have huge influence on the world not just America. After the war, many Italian singers were influenced by the greats of America and Italy was craving influence of new music. Many famous Rock and Roll stars even emerged from Italy or had Italian roots (Bensalhia). For example, Adriano Celentano was considered the Elvis of Italy. He did not merely copy the culture of the US but in a way created his own type of music specific to Italy ().

The Twist

The Twist itself is a dance that many are very familiar with. It became a worldwide phenomenon in the 1960s and the reason is not quite known why it became so big. The dance itself is based on Chubby Checker's song and dance that was featured on the Dick Clark Show in 1960. Due to the simplicity of the dance, many people were able to pick up the dance. The scene above in the video shows this fairly well. People who lived on farmers got together and danced the twist. They did not have a lot of money but they had each other and some time to relax and dance. Later in the film, a more "high class" party shows them also doing the twist just emphasizing its popularity with everyone (Rosenberg 2017). In a way, the popularity of this style of dance showed how much American had an influence on Italian culture.

Tango

This film shows a scene in which Mimi seduces Amalia with what seems to be the Tango. This dance style seems to have controversial questions as to where it originated and how it has routes in Italy. While the tango has a huge basis in Argentina, it seems to have captivated Italians for years. This could be due to the fact that many Italians had immigrated to Argentina in the late 1800s and early 1900s. Despite this film taking place in the 1970s the fact that Italy was still entranced by it seems to show just how lasting of a dance type it is. To this day, it is still danced and appreciated by Italians. In Serra's paper he states "The sentimentality that flows from tango melody derives from the feeling of nostalgia and longing for broken families and distant homes." This in itself is quite a beautiful statement and goes well with how this dance both captivates Mimi and Amalia and shows her longing for love.
